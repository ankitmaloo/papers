Why looped training blows up, and how Parcae controls it

Parcae is useful because it turns a vague training problem into something you can inspect. If the recurrence expands the residual state, repeated loops amplify the expansion. The paper constrains the part of the model that causes that behavior.

Failure signal
loss spikes

Runs can look fine early, then destabilize once recurrent residuals grow.

Diagnosis
spectral norm

The linearized recurrence exposes which learned terms can expand state.

Training fix
per-sequence R

Each sequence samples its own depth while the batch pads to the maximum.

01

The failure is visible during training

Residual explosion
paper observation
Prior looped models can train for a while and then hit loss spikes. The residual state grows across recurrences, and training becomes unstable.

The paper describes two related problems: residual state explosion and loss spikes. The obvious story would be “shared layers are hard to train.” Parcae gives a more specific diagnosis: the recurrence contains injection parameters that can learn an expansive map.

Once a map expands the state, recurrence makes the effect compound. A small expansion per loop becomes a large norm shift after many loops. This is why a looped model can look stable at low recurrence and fail at higher recurrence.

model

02

The recurrence as a system

mechanism
Parcae treats the recurrent block as a dynamical system with learned input injection, then constrains the expansive part.

Parcae writes the loop in a form close to:

ht+1 = A ht + B e + R(ht, e)

h is the residual state, e is the input signal re-injected into the loop, A and B are learned injection terms, and R is the nonlinear transformer block.

The linearized view gives the paper something concrete to measure. If the spectral radius of the recurrent part crosses the stability boundary, the state can diverge. The nonlinear block still matters, but the linear analysis explains why some trained runs learn unstable behavior.

What the animation is showing

The right pane uses rho as a one-number stand-in for expansion. Below 1, repeated application settles. Above 1, the state grows across loops. Parcae is about making the stable case natural for the optimizer.

03

The negative diagonal parameterization

Architecture
architecture move
The stability control is built into the parameterization, so the recurrent update starts from a safer family of maps.

Parcae parameterizes the injection dynamics through a negative diagonal form and then discretizes it. The point is structural: the model is pushed toward a stable update rule before training gets a chance to discover a bad one.

This is different from clipping a bad run after the fact. The architecture changes which recurrent maps are easy for the optimizer to represent. That is why the paper can talk about stable looping as a design property.

With Parcae

The injection parameterization constrains the spectral norm and reduces residual explosion.

04

Per-sequence depth sampling

training objective
The model has to learn a range of depths. Sampling depth per sequence gives the batch a richer depth distribution.

The training algorithm matters too. If every sequence in a micro-batch uses the same sampled recurrence depth, the gradient sees a narrow slice of the depth distribution at each step. Parcae samples depth per sequence and pads computation to the maximum depth in the micro-batch.

The paper reports that this reduces loss spikes. It also improves behavior at lower test-time recurrences. That is useful because a deployed model may exit early for easy tokens.

batch depth

One sampled depth for the whole micro-batch. Cheaper to think about, more brittle in the reported training curves.

sequence depth

Each sequence gets its own sampled recurrence depth. Shorter sequences hold state while longer ones keep looping.

05

Scaling laws after stability

scaling result
Once the loop is stable, recurrence becomes a compute axis that can be traded against data and evaluated with isoFLOP curves.

Once training is stable, Parcae studies looping as a compute axis. The paper's isoFLOP experiments show an optimum recurrence level at a fixed FLOP budget. Increasing loops without enough data is wasteful; increasing data without enough recurrence leaves compute on the table.

The clean result is that recurrence behaves like an axis you can scale alongside data. That is why Parcae matters beyond its architecture: it gives looped training a scaling story instead of a set of hand-tuned recipes.

06

What to carry forward

checklist
A looped training recipe needs stable dynamics, recurrence-aware sampling, and scaling experiments that vary loops and data together.
  • Stability is a model property. The loop must be stable across recurrence and across the depths you plan to run.
  • Sampling matters. Variable depth is part of the objective, so the sampling scheme changes the optimization problem.
  • Scaling needs both loops and data. Parcae's isoFLOP result argues for coordinated scaling.
